§ 01 — Context

The register hadn't been touched in eight years.

A regional Victorian public hospital planning a new oncology wing needed to demonstrate — to Department of Health, to the head contractor, and to the incoming clinical team — that the existing asbestos register reflected the actual state of every surface a builder or a patient might touch.

The most recent survey on file was from 2016. It had been produced by a removalist as part of a targeted works package, not as an independent audit. Large sections of the site — the boiler house, the older ward block, several service risers — carried "presumed asbestos" flags with no sampling behind them. The 2017 OHS Regulations register format wasn't in use. Photograph coverage was sparse.

The hospital's facilities manager engaged BENSS to re-audit every building, sample everything reasonably practicable, deliver a fresh compliant register, and remain retained through the build's clearance program.

§ 03 — Methodology

Live-site sampling around a running hospital.

Sampling in an operating hospital is not sampling in an empty building. Windows for access were 90-minute night blocks, coordinated week-by-week with nursing unit managers, infection control, and the on-call biomedical engineer. Every disturbance was contained under negative pressure. Every room was reinstated cleared and re-cleaned before shift handover.

“No fibre count above 0.01 f/mL in any occupied area for the entire program.”
